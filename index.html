Significant Figures Calculator – Rules, Examples, and Guide

Significant figures, often called sig figs, are a way of expressing numbers with precision. They help scientists, students, and professionals ensure that results reflect the accuracy of measurements.

Why Significant Figures Matter

In science and math, it is not enough to write down raw numbers. A value like 12.300 carries more information about accuracy than simply 12.3. Significant figures prevent us from overstating precision and keep results consistent.

Basic Rules of Significant Figures

1. Non-zero digits

All non-zero digits (1–9) are always significant. Example: 123 has three sig figs.

2. Leading zeros

Zeros before non-zero digits are not significant. Example: 0.0045 has two sig figs.

3. Captive zeros

Zeros between non-zero digits are significant. Example: 1002 has four sig figs.

4. Trailing zeros

Zeros after a decimal are significant, but trailing zeros without a decimal may be ambiguous. Example: 45.00 has four sig figs.

How Rules Apply in Calculations

Addition and Subtraction

Round to the least number of decimal places among the values.

Example: 12.3 + 1.45 = 13.75 → round to one decimal place → 13.8.

Multiplication and Division

Round to the least number of significant figures among the values.

Example: 2.15 × 3.4 = 7.31 → least SF is 2 → 7.3.
